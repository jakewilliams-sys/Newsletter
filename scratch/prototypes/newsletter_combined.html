We chose Maria because her story is one we're seeing more often in the data — but rarely get to hear in someone's own words. She's a high-frequency Plus Gold member who, on paper, looks like a retention success. Two hundred and forty-seven lifetime orders. Subscribed for two years. The kind of customer most teams would file under "healthy" and move on.

But look closer and something interesting is happening. Her behaviour is shifting. Not in the way that triggers a churn alert — in a subtler, more human way. And it raises a question our Plus Gold research has been circling all quarter:

What does loyalty actually look like when a customer grows up?

Chapter 1
The evening ritual

Maria's week has a rhythm. Most of it is quiet — a coffee here, an occasional lunch order. But every evening, somewhere between 7 and 11pm, something switches on. The kitchen stays dark. The app comes out.

Forty-eight orders from a single restaurant. That's not convenience — that's a relationship. Maria doesn't browse. She knows what she wants, and the platform's job is to get out of the way.

Chapter 2
Something changed

Six months ago, Maria was ordering 4.9 times a week. Nearly every day. That number has been falling steadily since — not dramatically, but consistently. The kind of drift that looks like noise week to week, but tells a clear story over months.

She's ordering less, but spending more each time. Her frequency dropped 16%, but her basket grew 10%. She's not disengaging — she's becoming more deliberate. Less impulse, more intent.

"I used to order every single night. Now it's more like four times a week — I started cooking on Sundays and meal prepping. But when I do order, I order properly."

Chapter 3
The moment she almost left

Here's where Maria's story gets interesting for us. Last month, she opened the app settings and hovered over "Cancel subscription." She'd been paying £7.99 a month for Plus Gold and wondered, with fewer orders, whether it still made sense.

This is the evaluation moment — the exact inflection point our Plus Gold acquisition research identified this quarter. The point where high-frequency users who've slowed down reassess whether the maths still works.

"I almost cancelled Plus last month, honestly. Not because it's bad — I just wondered if I still order enough to justify it. Then I checked and I'd saved £34 that month. So, yeah. Staying."

— Maria

She checked. The savings were real. Here's what her quarter looks like:

Even ordering less than she used to, Plus Gold saves Maria over £65 a quarter. The subscription pays for itself three times over. And that realisation — that moment of checking — is what kept her.

"I don't think about delivery fees anymore. That's the whole point. I'll order a coffee and a pastry on a Saturday morning just because I can. I never would have done that before Plus."

— Maria

Plus Gold hasn't just removed a fee — it's removed a decision. The mental barrier of "is this order worth the delivery charge?" no longer exists. That's why she orders a coffee on Saturday morning. That's why a 4.1×/week customer is arguably more valuable than a 4.9× one — she's ordering across more occasions, with higher intent and lower friction.

The bigger picture
Why Maria matters

Maria isn't an outlier. She's a pattern. Our Plus Gold acquisition barriers research found that high-frequency members who slow down are the most likely to hit the evaluation moment — and the ones who check their savings are the ones who stay.

The question for us isn't "how do we stop Maria from slowing down" — it's "how do we make sure the value is visible when she looks for it." Right now, she had to find it herself. What if we showed her?
